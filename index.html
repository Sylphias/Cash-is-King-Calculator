Indicators

Gross Margin

Gross margin percentage measures the relationship between sales revenue and cost of sales and indicates the portion of a dollar of sales that contributes to overall profit.

Formula : [(Revenue - COGS)/ Revenue] x 100

Net Profit before Interest & Tax (EBIT) Margin

EBIT margin measure the relationship between sales revenue and earnings before interest expense and tax and indicate the portion of a dollar of sales that contributes to overall earnings before tax.

Formula : [(Revenue - COGS - Operating & Other Expenses)/ Revenue] x 100

Net Profit before Interest & Tax, Depreciation and Amortization (EBITDA) Margin

EBITDA margin measure the operating profitability as a percentage of its total revenue. EBITDA can be used to analyze and compare profitability between companies and industries because it eliminates the effects of financing and accounting decisions, e.g. Depreciation.

Formula : [(Revenue - COGS - Operating & Other Expenses + Non-Cash Expenses)/ Revenue] x 100

Investment in Working Capital - per $1 EBITDA

Working capital Per EBITDA shows the amount of dollars which would be held as working capital for one dollar of EBITDA. A lower positive value is better.

Formula : [(Accounts Receivables + Inventory - Accounts Payables )/(EBITDA x 12/ Time period )]

Marginal Cash Flow per EBITDA

Marginal cash flow per EBITDA shows the amount of dollars of marginal cash flow that are necessary to generate one dollar of EBITDA, i.e. cash surplus or deficit after paying operating expenses and funding working capital requirements. A higher positive value is better.

Formula : (Revenue - COGS - Accounts Receivables - Inventory + Accounts Payables)/(EBITDA x 12)/ Time period]

Days Sales Outstanding

Days sales outstanding calculates how many days’ receivables are held before collected. It measures the efficiency of a company’s credit policies.A lower number of days sales outstanding is better.

Formula : [Accounts Receivables /(Revenue x 12 / Time period)] x 365

Days Inventory Outstanding

Days inventory outstanding calculates how many days inventory are held (on average) before it is sold. It measures the efficiency of a company’s inventory management. A lower number of days inventory outstanding is better.

Formula : [Inventory /(COGS x 12 / Time period)] x 365

Days Payables Outstanding

It is a measure of the average number of days the company takes to pay its vendors. A lower number of days payable outstanding shows that the vendors are being paid on a timely manner.

Formula : : {Accounts Payables /[(COGS + Operating & Other Expenses + Non-Cash Expenses) x 12 / Time period]} x 365

Cash Conversion Cycle

Cash convention cycle provides a snapshot of the company’s ability to turn accounts receivable and inventory into cash, less payments to vendors. A lower number of days is better.

Formula : Days Sales Outstanding + Days Inventory Outstanding - Days Payables Outstanding

Cash Flow Effects

Decrease COGS

Changes in cash flow when COGS is reduced. A reduction in COGS would improve cash flow position positively.

Formula : (COGS + Inventory - Accounts Payable) x % Change

Reduce Operating & Other Expenses

Changes in cash flow when operating & other expenses is reduced. A reduction in operating & other expenses would improve cash flow position positively.

Formula : (Overheads + Non-Cash Expense) x % Change

Increase Sales Price

Changes in cash flow when selling price is increased. An increase in selling price would improve cash flow position positively.

Formula : (Revenue - Accounts Receivable) x % Change

Increase Sales Volume

Changes in cash flow when sales volume is increased. An increase in sales volume would improve cash flow position positively.

Formula : (Revenue - COGS - Accounts Receivable - Inventory + Accounts Payable) x % Change

Reduce Debtors

Changes in cash flow when debt collection days are shortened. A reduction in debt collection days would improve cash flow position positively.

Formula : Accounts Receivable - [(Days Sales Outstanding - Days Change) x (Revenue / 365) x (12/ Time period) ]

Reduce Inventory

Changes in cash flow when inventory turnover days are shortened. A reduction in inventory turnover days would improve cash flow position positively.

Formula : Inventory - [(Days Inventory Outstanding - Days Change) x (COGS / 365) x (12/ Time period) ]

Increase Creditors

Changes in cash flow when inventory turnover days are lengthened. An increase in creditors days would improve cash flow position positively.

Formula : Accounts Payable - {(Days Payable Outstanding - Days Change) x [(COGS + Other Operating Expenses - Non-Cash Expenses) / 365] x (12 / Time period)}
